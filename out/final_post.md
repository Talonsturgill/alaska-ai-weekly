"Three federal decisions landed this week affecting Alaska's energy, ocean science, and AI policy, and none of them waited for Alaska to have a position ready.

The Trump DOE committed $89 million on June 4 to study the viability of a proposed 1.25-gigawatt coal-fired plant in the Susitna River watershed, west of Skwentna. The developer is Terra Energy Center Corp., an affiliate of Canadian firm Flatlands Energy, with the Mat-Su Borough partnering on the project. The explicit pitch, per the DOE's own fact sheet, is that surplus power from the plant would feed AI data centers. That's a plausible story to tell in Washington. It's harder to tell in Alaska.

There is no mine. There's no transmission line. There's no power-purchase agreement. This is $89 million to study whether a coal plant at this remote site is worth building.

Then NSF announced it's pulling three deep-sea sensor moorings from the Gulf of Alaska, the network known as Ocean Station Papa. UAF oceanographer Russ Hopcroft told Alaska Public Media it's "an unfortunate time to have something being pulled out of the water," given forecasts of a super El Niño for summer 2026. Those sensors feed the fisheries data used to set Gulf of Alaska catch limits. One federal agency is spending $89 million to study coal for AI. Another just cut the ocean monitoring that Alaska's fishing industry depends on.

On the regulatory side, House members introduced the Great American Artificial Intelligence Act on June 4, according to Roll Call. The 269-page draft would preempt state AI regulations for three years. Alaska's own joint legislative AI task force resolution, HCR 3, did not advance during the 2026 session, according to a June 3 ADN letter from Roger Kaye of Fairbanks. As AFA Council 30 president LeiLauni Scheideman wrote in an ADN op-ed on June 1, the stakes for Alaska workers are real, and a three-year federal preemption clock doesn't pause while Alaska figures out what it wants to say.

What would it actually take for Alaska's institutions to be at the table before these federal decisions are made, rather than reacting to them after?"
