# Alaska.Ai — VOL. 01 — 10 MAY 2026

“The Air Force wants AI data center proposals at three Alaska bases by May 29 — nine days after the legislature is constitutionally required to go home.

The Anchorage Daily News reported May 5 that the Department of the Air Force is soliciting proposals to build up to 12 data centers across 4,700 acres at Joint Base Elmendorf-Richardson, Eielson AFB, and Clear Space Force Station. In the same piece, Chugach Electric Association and Golden Valley Electric Association told ADN they don’t currently have the gas to power a large data center. Golden Valley’s Cook Inlet share fell from roughly 30 percent to under 1 percent of its fuel mix in a single year, per ADN. Chugach’s Hilcorp contract expires in 2028.

Last week this desk argued the legislature’s incentives-versus-moratorium binary was structurally false. This week the problem has gotten worse — it’s also a sequence problem. Federal procurement clocks do not wait for state policy windows. Whatever ratepayer-protection framework — or vacuum — exists when the gavel falls on May 20 is what greets the first wave of federal-backed proposals nine days later.

The piece of policy that would matter most is SB 250, Sen. Loki Tobin’s bill to bar data center costs from being passed to existing ratepayers. It sat in Senate Labor & Commerce without a hearing scheduled as of late April, according to KTUU; a hearing is reportedly set for May 13 per BillTrack50. That gives the committee one week to move a cost-allocation framework that the federal solicitation does not need to wait for.

The contrast underneath all of this is what the federal side is already building around. On May 8, UAF and the Pentagon’s Defense Innovation Unit signed a three-year MOU — announced alongside Sen. Sullivan, Sen. Murkowski, and DIU Director Owen West — naming ACUASI on drones and ACEP on energy resilience as lead nodes, with hypersonics, remote sensing, and space systems to follow. That is federal capital compounding on existing Alaska expertise. It is the third path the legislature keeps not naming. The same week, the House advanced HB 217 — Rep. Ashley Carrick’s autonomous-vehicle bill requiring a human safety operator on board — proving the body can move tech policy when it wants to. It is moving on the wrong tech for this moment.

If the May 20 gavel falls with SB 250 still in committee, the rules that govern the May 29 proposals get written somewhere other than Juneau. Is that the sequence Alaskans want, or is one week enough time to put a ratepayer floor under what comes next?”
