"Alaska's legislature closed its regular session on May 20th with a split verdict on AI, and the gap between the two halves is what the press coverage mostly missed.

Lawmakers passed a tough, bipartisan bill criminalizing AI-generated child sexual abuse material. HB 239 cleared the Senate 20-0 on May 19th and the House with overwhelming margins the following day, and it now awaits the governor's signature despite his office's concerns about deliberation speed and potential legal problems. By most accounts, that's a win.

That framing is incomplete. The missing half is the one that costs ratepayers money. SB 250, sponsored by Sen. Löki Tobin, would have required data centers to pay the grid upgrade costs they create, instead of passing those costs to ordinary utility customers. The Senate passed it 14-5 on May 16th. The House Community and Regional Affairs committee heard it on May 18th and held it. Two days later, session ended. No state law now governs who pays when a data center plugs into the Railbelt grid. That is not a procedural footnote. That is an open financial obligation with no closing date.

The two bills aren't separate issues. HB 239 and SB 250 are both answers to the same question. What does Alaska owe the communities it's about to transform with AI infrastructure? One answer was easy because the harm is unambiguous. The other answer required telling a potential industry that it pays its own way, and the House didn't get there.

This matters now because the governor called a special session on May 21st, focused entirely on LNG pipeline tax structure. Three competing proposals are in play. The governor's runs 6 cents volumetric. The House substitute goes to 15 cents and adds a Fairbanks spur line and a $40 million community impact fund. The Senate's runs 40 cents. The Alaska LNG pipeline is the project most likely to address the gas supply gap. Chugach Electric and Golden Valley Electric told ADN in early May that Cook Inlet supply is already too thin for large-scale compute.

The Air Force's proposal deadline for data centers at JBER, Eielson, and Clear SFS lands on May 29th.

Alaska's legislature agreed on what AI can't do to people. It didn't agree on what AI infrastructure should cost them. Which matters more to you, what the legislature agreed on or what it left unresolved?"
