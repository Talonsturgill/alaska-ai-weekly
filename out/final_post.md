“Three federal AI threads landed in Alaska this week, and they don’t add up to a coherent deal.

On May 9, Sen. Dan Sullivan announced that UAF and the Pentagon’s Defense Innovation Unit signed a three-year agreement covering energy, hypersonics, drone operations, remote sensing, and space systems. UAF leadership floated a DIU hub on campus, geothermal power for nearby military bases, and drone research at minus 40 degrees. Sullivan called Alaska ‘one of the most strategically important places in the world for America’s national defense.’ Four days earlier, ADN’s Alex DeMarban reported the Department of the Air Force is offering up to 12 AI data-center sites across roughly 4,700 acres at JBER, Clear SFS, and Eielson, with proposals due May 29. The Senate Labor and Commerce Committee picks up Sen. Löki Tobin’s data-center bill on May 13.

The conventional read is that this is a good week for Alaska. Federal money, federal partners, federal infrastructure, all pointing at the 49th. The structural problem got buried under the announcements.

On May 8, U.S. District Judge Colleen McMahon ruled the Trump administration’s cancellation of more than 1,400 National Endowment for the Humanities grants unconstitutional, specifically because DOGE used ChatGPT to flag grants as ‘DEI’ and terminate them. McMahon wrote that ‘DOGE’s use of AI to identify DEI-related material neither excuses presumptively unconstitutional conduct nor gives the Government carte blanche to engage in it.’ Per a deposition unsealed in March, one of the grants the algorithm flagged and killed was bound for UAF’s Alaska Native Language Center. The same university that just signed onto the federal defense-AI pipeline.

Hosting federal AI capacity is one transaction. Governing the federal AI decisions made about Alaska is a different one. This week, Alaska said yes to the first and got a court ruling that the second isn’t being done lawfully. The Tobin bill is the only piece of in-state machinery that touches the governance question at all, and it’s scoped to ratepayers and benefit agreements, not to how a federal agency uses a chatbot to decide which Alaskan institutions are worth funding.

That’s the lock-in. Sign the host-side deals first and figure out the governance later, and Alaska carries federal AI infrastructure on its grid with no standing in the federal AI decisions that touch its language programs, its grants, and its university.

If Alaska is going to host the federal government’s AI buildout, what should the state demand in return on how that AI gets used on Alaskans?”
