Off Anchorage, one of the rarest whales on Earth just got two new watchers.

This summer NOAA switched on two AI systems to find Cook Inlet's belugas. One listens: an open-source deep-learning model that reads the noisy inlet and picks a single beluga call out of the vessel traffic. One looks: a program called GAIA that runs very-high-resolution satellite imagery through computer vision to spot the white smudge of a whale from orbit. By ear and by eye, on the same animal, for the first time.

Here's why it lands hard. About 331 Cook Inlet belugas are left. There were roughly 1,300 in 1979. That's a fall of nearly 80 percent, and the population is still listed as endangered.

So a sharper count is not a small thing. You cannot protect what you cannot even see. But watching is not saving. Better eyes do not quiet the boats or bring back the salmon these whales need. What the AI actually hands Alaska is one hard, open, timestamped number: 331. Whether that becomes a floor the state and tribes defend in the next Cook Inlet decision, or just a better-documented countdown, is a call no machine makes.

The most interesting part is who now owns the number. The acoustic model is open-source, and Alaska scientists at NOAA are central to it. Should a live beluga count carry real weight in Anchorage-area port, vessel, and development permitting, or is a precise number just a cleaner record of a population running out?
