Alaska's wildfire smoke forecast has a quiet flaw: on the worst days it reads the air up to five times too clean.

That is the finding in a new paper from the UAF Geophysical Institute, and what the team did about it is the part worth sitting with.

NOAA's HRRR-Smoke model forecasts the wildfire smoke people breathe at ground level. In Alaska it tends to undercount that surface smoke, where the lungs are, by up to five times on the heaviest days. The reason is vertical. A warm lid of air, a temperature inversion, settles over a valley and traps the smoke down low. The model keeps drawing it thin and high.

Jingqiu Mao and doctoral student Zhiwei Dong trained a second model, a convolutional neural network, to learn that bias and pull the forecast back down toward the measured air. It works. The undercount shrinks from about five times to about two.

The restraint is what earns respect. They did not claim to fix the smoke. A correction is not a cure. The network sharpens the number, it cannot move the air, and when a cloud hides a fire's heat the model can still miss it. A person still decides when it is safe to breathe.

That is the right posture for AI in a place like Alaska. Not a system that replaces the judgment, one that hands a sharper number to the person already making the call, and hands it sooner. With Alaska's 2026 fire season running hot again, sooner matters.

Where else should we want AI to correct the tool instead of replacing the expert?

Paper: ACS ES&T Air, 2026, 3(5).
