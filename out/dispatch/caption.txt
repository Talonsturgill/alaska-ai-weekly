A cruise ship docked in Seward on June 21 with a dead, pregnant fin whale draped across its bow.

The preliminary necropsy found injuries consistent with a vessel strike. The official cause is still pending.

Separately, on Matson's Alaska cargo route, an AI system called WhaleSpotter has been coming online.

It reads heat instead of shape. A whale's body runs warmer than the sea around it, and the camera catches that difference from about three nautical miles out, day or night. Matson says the system flags a whale in time to turn more than nine times in ten. That is the company's own number, not an independent test.

No Alaska cruise ship is known to carry anything like it. Nobody has reported offering Royal Caribbean this system, and nobody has reported it turning one down.

Here is the position worth arguing with: Alaska should require it. If thermal detection is coming online on Matson's freighters, it should become a licensing condition for every cruise ship transiting Alaska's whale corridors, not a courtesy some operators adopt and others skip.

The AI cannot steer a ship built for four thousand passengers or overrule a captain. It only puts one fact on a screen: something is out there. Somebody still has to act on it. Right now that fact only exists on some of the vessels in the water.

NOAA already requires observer or electronic-monitoring coverage on Alaska's groundfish fleet. Should the equivalent for whales be mandatory too?
