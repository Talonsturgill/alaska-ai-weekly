Post text (copy/paste)

Alaska was asked if it wanted a gigawatt AI data center on the North Slope. Over 500 people answered. Fewer than a dozen said yes.

Here is what is on the table near Deadhorse: one gas-fired campus on a square mile of state land, drawing at least a gigawatt. That is roughly 30 percent above all of urban Alaska's peak power demand. For one building.

The economics should give everyone pause. The company's own figures run from about 500 million dollars for initial site work, per the lease documents, to over 10 billion for the full build, per its own chief strategy officer. That is a 20 times spread, on a project with no confirmed customers and no confirmed chips. Nothing is built. Nothing is approved. The state has not decided.

What has happened is that Alaskans showed up. The public comment record is not close: 500 plus comments logged, fewer than a dozen in favor. Some were blunt enough to quote. "HELL NO to any data centers." "NO AI."

I do not read that as Alaska being anti technology. I read it as a simple rule: when a project this big arrives with numbers this unsettled, the burden of proof sits with the applicant, not the public. And the mechanism that actually holds that burden, the public comment window, closes tomorrow. July 17, 4:30 pm Alaska time, with Alaska DNR's Division of Oil and Gas (ADL 422741).

The loudest thing on the tundra right now is not the machine. It is the mailbox.

If a company wanted a gigawatt on your grid, with a 20 times cost range and no named customer, what would your comment say?
