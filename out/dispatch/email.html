The Air Force just offered the AI industry 4,700 acres of Alaska military land. The turbines to feed it are priced up by that industry.

That loop is the whole story, and Alaska is the one holding the switch.

The offer covers 12 parcels at JBER, Eielson, and Clear Space Force Station for commercial AI data centers. Per KTUU, proposals closed June 29. No awards have been announced. Nothing is built.

Here is the gap. A large data center can consume the electricity of roughly 100,000 households, and the utility expected to serve the Eielson-area parcels is already strained. Cook Inlet gas fell from about 30% of Golden Valley Electric Association's fuel supply to under 1% last year, per ADN. At points last winter, GVEA burned at least 250,000 gallons of diesel a day to keep the lights on.

GVEA's answer is a roughly $120 million turbine, which its board takes up in a July 13 executive session. Why $120 million? Turbine construction costs are up 195% since 2019, with waitlists into the early 2030s, per a Bloomberg report cited by the Fairbanks Daily News-Miner. The driver of that inflation: data centers demanding guaranteed power.

So the ask arrives with its own price hike attached. Alaska would be paying the boom's prices to feed the boom.

That is not a rescue. It is a negotiation. And Alaska holds real cards: the land, the cold, and the choice to say no. The neighbors are weighing it too. A North Pole-area resident told KTUU: "People say living close to them, it's like having a vacuum cleaner on 24 hours a day, seven days a week." Noise, water, and rates come before megawatts on their list.

What terms should Alaska demand before saying yes: developer-funded generation, rate protection for existing customers, something else?
