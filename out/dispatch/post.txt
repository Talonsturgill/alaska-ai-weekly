Right now a volcano is erupting in Alaska, and no one can stand on it to watch.

Great Sitkin is oozing lava at this moment, held at the Alaska Volcano Observatory's WATCH and ORANGE alert as of July 8. It sits in the Aleutians, on a chain of dozens of volcanoes, most of them hundreds of miles from the nearest person. You cannot post a scientist on every summit. So Alaska does the next best thing. It listens.

Every rumble sends sound through the rock, and pressure waves through the air. AVO turns that sound into a picture, a spectrogram, and a deep-learning model called VOISS-Net learns to read it. Trained on more than 270,000 spectrograms from 7 volcanoes, it can tell tremor from an explosion, a quake from plain noise. On its test set it was right about 87 percent of the time.

The limits are the point.

VOISS-Net does not predict eruptions. It can name the sound the mountain is making right now. It cannot tell you the hour it will blow. Push it past the signals it was trained on and it slips. So it does not replace the duty scientist watching the screens. It gives that person a faster ear.

That is the version of AI worth trusting in a place with real stakes. Not a model that claims to see the future, but a classifier that knows the edge of what it knows, wired to a human who owns the call.

A volcano you cannot stand on is a clean test of that idea. So is an ICU, a power grid, a harbor.

Where else in Alaska should the standard be AI that listens and flags, with the final call left to a person on watch?
