A drone lifts off over Alaska's Wood River at 8:30 each morning to film the sockeye run.

It cannot count a single fish yet. A person has to teach it first, one fish at a time.

For over 70 years, Alaska has counted its salmon by hand. People sit in wooden towers on the riverbank and click a counter, 10 minutes of every hour, around the clock. That number sets the season for the largest sockeye run on Earth. It tells the fleet when to fish and when to stop.

Now a pilot project on the Wood River is testing whether a drone and a machine-learning model can help. The drone flies a fixed path and films the run from above. Then the real work starts.

A technician marks each fish that crosses a line on the screen, frame by frame, several thousand of them, so the model can learn what a sockeye looks like. Only after that can it start to pick fish out on its own.

I keep coming back to that part. We talk about AI as if it arrives finished. It does not. Behind almost every model that "just works" is a person doing patient, unglamorous labeling that nobody sees.

And the honest limits are still there. The model has not been tested against the tower counts yet. A shadow on the water can still fool it. So this summer, the counters still climb the towers, and the model learns to watch beside them.

That is the version of AI in Alaska I trust most: taught before it is trusted. Not a replacement for the people who know the river. A tool that has to be taught by them first.

What is the human labor hiding underneath the "automated" system you rely on?
