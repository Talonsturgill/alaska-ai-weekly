Alaska is proposing to hand a state agency 31 square miles of public forest, for free, before a single company has signed on.

The state Department of Natural Resources issued a preliminary decision to convey about 19,950 acres near Houston, in the Mat-Su Borough, to AIDEA, the state's industrial development authority, at no charge. The framing is an industrial and energy district. AIDEA's stated vision includes data centers, an Alaska Railroad freight hub, energy corridors, and advanced manufacturing pads.

Data centers are the physical body of the AI boom. Racks, power, cooling, and a great deal of land. So this one is worth sitting with. A public forest is being lined up as a landing pad for that buildout, and right now there is no named operator, no stated price, and no final decision on how any of it would actually be used.

Two honest caveats keep this from tipping into alarm. AIDEA says no land use decisions have been made, and the DNR notice itself does not name data centers. Public comment stays open through August 19. Nothing here is signed.

The detail that stays with me is a jurisdictional one. Houston's own council moved to weigh in, but the parcel sits about two miles outside the city, past the line where its authority runs out. Roughly 42 miles of mushing and recreation trails cross that land either way.

So the question is not whether AI needs somewhere to live. It plainly does. The question is what public land is worth, and who gets to speak for it, when the giveaway arrives before the plan.

If your state offered free public land to court a data center that has no operator yet, would you call that smart investment or a gamble with the commons?
