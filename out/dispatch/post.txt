A caribou count that ties up biologists for weeks just got done by an AI, and it landed within about 3 percent of the herd.

It learns each animal from a single dot, instead of thousands of hand-drawn boxes.

The model is called OWL, out of Microsoft's AI for Good Lab. Instead of learning from a hand-drawn box around each animal, it learns from one point. The paper notes those point labels are up to seven times faster and three times cheaper to produce than boxes.

On the Alaska Department of Fish and Game's 2022 Central Arctic Herd survey, its count came within about 3 percent of the herd. An F1 of 0.965. For a task that ties up biologists for weeks, that is a real shift.

Here is what I respect about the work. The researchers are blunt about where it breaks.

Accuracy is regime dependent. Where the animals crowd together, the count starts to slip, and the heaviest foundation-model version actually does worse on the densest herds than a simpler one. It counts what the camera sees. It cannot fly the plane, and it cannot find the herd.

That framing matters for Alaska. The Central Arctic and Porcupine herds are subsistence and management cornerstones, and the people who fly those surveys are not being replaced. The plane still flies. The model turns a photo backlog into a count that comes home sooner and costs less.

The most useful AI in the field right now is not the one that promises to replace the expert. It is the one that takes the tedious, high-volume work, tells you exactly where it is weak, and hands the judgment back.
