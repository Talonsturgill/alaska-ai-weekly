# Selection — VOL. 02 — 23 MAY 2026

## Mode
**Deep Dive.** One structural tension dominates: Alaska's legislature closed its AI policy window on May 20 with a split verdict — unanimous on protecting children from AI exploitation (HB 239), silent on protecting ratepayers from AI infrastructure costs (SB 250 dead) — and then immediately opened a special session whose outcome will determine whether Alaska can power the data centers it's been debating all session.

## Lead
**The legislature's final AI scorecard — and what it skipped.**

On the final day of the regular session, HB 239 passed 20-0 in the Senate and 39-1 in the House. The omnibus crime bill includes Alaska's first felony statute for AI-generated child sexual abuse material and a $1 million civil penalty per incident on AI platforms. Governor Dunleavy has not yet signed it.

Two days before that vote, SB 250 was heard and held in the House Community and Regional Affairs Committee on May 18. The bill — which would have required electric and gas utilities to recover data center costs from the data centers, not from ordinary ratepayers — had passed the Senate 14-5-1 on May 16. It died without a House floor vote at the May 20 adjournment. No replacement. No framework. Nothing in state law governs who pays when a large AI data center plugs into the Railbelt grid.

## Supporting beats

1. **LNG special session as the energy backdrop.** The legislature adjourned its regular session and immediately convened a special session on tax breaks for the $46 billion Alaska LNG pipeline. Both Chugach Electric Association and Golden Valley Electric Association have said Cook Inlet gas supply is too depleted to power a large data center. North Slope gas via a new pipeline is the only plausible alternative at scale. The special session's outcome — not any AI-specific legislation — will determine whether Stak Energy's 3-gigawatt North Slope proposal or the Air Force's solicitation for 12 data centers at JBER, Eielson, and Clear SFS can be physically powered.

2. **HB 217 and SB 287 also dead.** The AV bill (HB 217), which would have established Alaska's first regulatory framework for commercial autonomous vehicles, died after a 2-do-not-pass, 1-do-pass Senate committee split on May 17. SB 287, which would have created a legislative task force to review state support for UAF's Alaska Native Language Center, was referred to Senate Rules on May 16 and never got a floor vote. These are supporting evidence of the pattern: Alaska's legislative AI agenda closed with one passage and several failures.

3. **Air Force May 29 deadline — upcoming context.** Background: the Air Force's solicitation for data centers at JBER, Eielson, and Clear SFS has a May 29 proposal deadline — six days from today. No proposals have been publicly confirmed. Power supply remains unresolved for those sites too. This is a live near-term watch item but not a new in-window development.

## Package angle

The lead frame for this week is **Alaska's legislature resolved the AI question it found easiest and skipped the one that matters for infrastructure.** HB 239 passed unanimously — everyone agrees AI-generated child exploitation is a felony. SB 250 died in committee — nobody could agree on whether data centers should pay their own power bills. And the energy question that determines whether any of Alaska's proposed AI infrastructure can actually run has now been handed to a special session that wasn't called to answer AI questions.

This is structurally different from prior frames:
- NOT "executive vs. legislative speed gap" (Vol. 03 frame — DNR lease vs. weak committee votes)
- NOT "hosting vs. governing" (Vol. 02 frame — UAF/DIU deal vs. NEH/ChatGPT grant cancellations)
- NOT "binary is false" (Vol. 01 anchor frame — incentives vs. moratorium)

This week's tension is **inside the legislature's own record**: it answered one category of AI question (exploitation) and refused to answer another (cost allocation), then passed the energy question to a separate session. The pattern is the story.
