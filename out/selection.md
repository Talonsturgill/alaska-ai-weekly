# Selection — VOL. 01 — 15 MAY 2026

## Mode
**Deep Dive.** One structural tension dominates: while the Alaska legislature spent the week trying to figure out how to govern data centers, the executive branch quietly approved the largest data center proposal in state history.

## Lead
**Stak Energy North Slope AI data center — DNR preliminary lease decision (May 13-14).**

A $500 million, one-square-mile lease on state land 25 miles south of major North Slope infrastructure. Up to 3 gigawatts. More than twice urban Alaska's total natural gas consumption for electricity and heat. The Alaska Department of Natural Resources moved this from speculative to a live 50-year lease application, with public comment open through June 15. Operations target 2028. 7 million cubic yards of gravel. Stak recently hired the governor's former natural resources commissioner.

## Supporting

1. **SB 250 (Tobin) clears Senate L&C 1-3 No Rec, placed on Senate calendar May 15.** Only three of four committee members would even decline to take a position; only one would endorse it. Statewide applicability to electric and gas utilities serving data centers. Five days before the May 20 gavel.

2. **HB 217 (Carrick) human-operator AV bill clears Senate Transportation 0-4 No Rec, held in State Affairs May 14.** Four legislators voted to move the bill without backing it. State Affairs declined to advance it on May 14.

3. **HB 239 omnibus crime bill containing AI-generated CSAM felony provisions heard and held in Senate Finance May 11.** AI-criminal-statute survival is now contingent on the omnibus package surviving.

## Package angle

The lead frame for this week is **the speed gap between Alaska's executive and legislative branches on AI infrastructure.** SB 250, HB 217, and HB 239 are all crawling through committees with weak votes (1 Do Pass / 3 No Rec; 0-4 No Rec; held without a vote) in the final week of session. Meanwhile, DNR — an executive-branch agency — issued a preliminary best-interest finding on a 50-year lease for a 3 GW AI data center, opening a public comment window that runs a month past the legislature's adjournment.

This is structurally new. Prior weeks framed the tension as federal-clock-versus-state-gavel (May 10 post) and host-deals-versus-governance-standing (May 11 post). This week's tension is **inside Alaska state government itself.** The executive branch is moving on AI infrastructure faster than the elected branch can decide whether or how to govern it. By May 20, the legislature will have a record of what it passed; DNR will already be six weeks into a public comment period.

SB 250's existence is not the same as SB 250's passage. The Stak Energy lease did not need SB 250 to advance. It needed DNR's signature. It got it.
