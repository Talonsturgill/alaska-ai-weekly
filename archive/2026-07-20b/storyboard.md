# The Referee Arrives (2026-07-20b)

Alaska's bitter salmon bycatch argument finally gets a machine that can count what nobody could agree on. The count that matters most is the one the Sitka Tribe of Alaska built for its own river.

## The film in one breath
A chrome salmon noses a fogged lens: WHO COUNTS THIS FISH? Pull back: two crowds shout mismatched numbers across a thin river (a raven steals a decimal). The costs are honest on both banks: quiet smokehouses upriver, a fleet that hates salmon in its nets, an ocean pressing the runs down. The old count: a human buried in paper, weeks deep. The new count: a brass counting head that reads 85,000 learned fish and tallies 97% of them in hours, stamped STILL RESEARCH. A Senate bill demands numbers everyone can trust. A referee steps into the river and blows the whistle: the clicker locks, the crowds go silent, and the camera booms up to show the truth: a clean count over an almost empty river. Referees settle arguments. They do not refill rivers. So who holds the counter? At Redoubt Lake, a Sitka Tribe of Alaska biologist plants her own video weir: their river, their count. The lens returns: WHO COUNTS THIS FISH? YOU DO.
