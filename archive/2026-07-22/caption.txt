The Air Force just offered 4,700 acres of Alaska land, across three bases, for AI data centers. Nobody has been picked yet.

Here is what is actually happening. Joint Base Elmendorf-Richardson, Eielson Air Force Base, and Clear Space Force Station have 12 parcels open to private companies through an Enhanced Use Lease. The Air Force is not selling this land. It keeps the land and gets at least fair market value, reportedly for up to 50 years. The deadline for proposals was June 29. As of today, no operator has been selected.

Robert Moriarty, who oversees Air Force installations, calls it "a unique opportunity for a true public-private partnership." He is probably right that it could be.

Sarah Hollister has lived on land near one of the eligible sites for three generations. On the noise, she says it would be "like having a vacuum cleaner on 24 hours a day, seven days a week."

Both of those things can be true at once. A real opportunity, and a real cost to someone's daily life.

What strikes me is not the decision. It is that there is not one yet. The lease is still open, and so is the argument for what should happen with it.

If Alaska takes this deal, what has to be true a decade from now for it to have actually been worth it for the people who live there, not just the companies leasing the land?
