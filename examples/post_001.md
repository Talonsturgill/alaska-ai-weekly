# VOL. 01 — Alaska.Ai — 10 May 2026

“Alaska’s legislature only has ten days left to decide on data centers, but there may be a third path worth adding to the conversation.

The National Conference of State Legislatures briefed lawmakers last week that they face a binary. Offer tax incentives to attract data centers, or impose a moratorium to block them by May 20th. The framing is politically convenient and structurally false.

It assumes the only AI infrastructure worth having requires hundreds of megawatts of continuous power, which is exactly the wrong assumption for a state whose utilities are warning that Cook Inlet gas supply will fall short of existing residential and commercial demand by next year, before the first data center shovel breaks ground. The incentive path fails on its own merits. There is no surplus power to attract anything to.

The third option nobody is naming is targeting AI workloads that don’t need hyperscale compute. Inference. Agentic systems. Vertical AI for sectors where Alaska already has deep domain expertise.

Defense, with JBER, Eielson, and Clear SFS already operating here. Healthcare, where Alaska has built rural telehealth and tribal health systems other states don’t have. Natural resources, where Alaska has fisheries, mining, and oil and gas data the rest of the country doesn’t.

These workloads run on tens of kilowatts, not hundreds of megawatts. They compete on expertise, not electricity. Alaska is one of the few places in the country with the domain knowledge to actually build them. The legislature is about to lock in a framing that assumes Alaska can only compete on cheap power, but the opposite may be true. The policy frame could be missing the entire opportunity.

Where do you think Alaska’s real AI advantage sits, in attracting outside compute, or in building on expertise we already have?”

## Structural pattern (preserve in future weeks)

1. **Hook (1 sentence)** — names a concrete deadline or live tension, hints at a missing option.
2. **Conventional framing (1 short paragraph)** — what the briefing / press / lawmakers / national press are saying.
3. **Counter-framing / "structurally false" move (1 paragraph)** — why the conventional take is incomplete; ground in numbers (Cook Inlet gas, existing demand).
4. **The third option / what's actually possible (1 paragraph + 1 list-style paragraph)** — the missing path, named with specifics (named bases, named sectors, named numbers).
5. **Stakes / lock-in moment (1 paragraph)** — what's about to be decided and why now matters.
6. **Engagement question (1 sentence)** — opens a real debate the reader can answer.

**Length:** ~340 words. **No hashtags. No "follow us" CTA. Curly quotes. Specific named entities throughout** (NCSL, May 20th, JBER, Eielson, Clear SFS, Cook Inlet).
